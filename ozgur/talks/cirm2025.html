How do you solve the equation \[ x^2 - 3x + 2 =0 \] in real numbers?

\[ \boxed{x^2 - 3x + 2 = (x-1)(x-2) = 0} \] This implies $x = 1$ or $x = 2$.

You use the fact that \[ab = 0 \iff a = 0 \text{ or } b= 0 \]

This fact does not work with matrices!

In the matrix world, \[ AB = 0 \iff \mathrm{col}(B) \subseteq \mathrm{null}(A) .\]

In the world of linear maps, \[f \circ g = 0 \iff \mathrm{im}(g) \subseteq \mathrm{ker}(f). \]

Question: What is the difference between $\mathrm{im}(g)$ and $\mathrm{ker}(f)$?

We look at the ``quotient" $H = \frac{\mathrm{ker}(f)}{\mathrm{im}(g)} $.

We have \[ H = 0 \iff \mathrm{im}(g) = \mathrm{ker}(f). \]

Let $R$ be a ring and $M$ be an $R$-module.

  • $M$ is finitely generated if it has finitely many generators,
  • $M$ is free if it is of the form $R^n$ for some $n$.

Not every finitely generated module is free.

However,
we can always find a (possibly infinite) sequence of free modules \[ \ldots \to F_{n+1} \xrightarrow{d_{n+1}} F_n \xrightarrow{d_n} F_{n-1} \to \ldots \to F_1 \xrightarrow{d_1} F_0 \xrightarrow{d_0 = 0} 0 \] such that $d_{n} \circ d_{n+1} = 0$ for all $n \geq 0$ and \[H_n = 0 \text{ for } n>0 \quad,\quad H_0 \cong M.\]

This is called a resolution of $M$.

Let $R$ be a ring and $M$ be an $R$-module.

  • $M$ is finitely generated if it has finitely many generators,
  • $M$ is free if it is of the form $R^n$ for some $n$.

Not every finitely generated module is free.

However, we can always find a (possibly infinite) sequence $\mathcal{F}$ of free modules \[ \ldots \to F_{n+1} \xrightarrow{d_{n+1}} F_n \xrightarrow{d_n} F_{n-1} \to \ldots \to F_1 \xrightarrow{d_1} F_0 \xrightarrow{d_0 = 0} 0 \] such that $d_{n} \circ d_{n+1} = 0$ for all $n \geq 0$ and \[H_n(\mathcal{F}) = 0 \text{ for } n>0 \quad,\quad H_0(\mathcal{F}) \cong M.\]

This is called a resolution of $M$.

Let $\mathcal{F}$ be a resolution of $M$.

If $g \colon F_{n-1} \to N$, then $g \circ d_n \colon F_n \to N$ which means we have a map \[\mathrm{Hom}_R(F_{n}, N) \xleftarrow{\partial_n} \mathrm{Hom}_R(F_{n-1}, N). \]

It is a fact that $\partial_{n+1} \circ \partial_n = 0$ for any $n \geq 0$.

BUT! We may now have homologies!

We put \[E^n(M,N) = H_n(\mathrm{Hom}_R(\mathcal{F}, N)) \]

What do I study?

  • I study annihilators of these $E^n(M,N)$.
  • They carry a lot of geometric information.
  • I study geometric properties of solutions sets of polynomials using this type of computations.

Nuggets

Let $a_1, \ldots, a_n$ be positive integers. Which numbers can you write as \[ m_1 a_1 + \cdots + m_n a_n \]where $m_1, \ldots, m_n$ are nonnegative integers?

Example: With $3$ and $5$, we can create \[\langle 3, 5 \rangle = \{0,3,5,6,8,9,10,\ldots \}\]

Keyword: Numerical semigroup.

The semigroup ring $k[t^3, t^5]$ consists of all polynomials in a variable $t$ which you can generate by $t^3$ and $t^5$.

In my thesis, I considered the problem of annihilators of ``Ext" for such rings.

I showed that the conductor ideal (in our example polynomials in $t^8, t^9, t^{10}, ...$) is exactly the ideal that annihilates all ``high" Ext - whenever the semigroup satisfies a certain symmetry condition.
